# The empty chair still casts a shadow (January 21, 2027)

USER: I need to rehearse something I’m going to say in a room where people measure sentences like they measure blood pressure. We’re finalizing governance for “automated decision support”—which is the phrase legal lets us use without implying we’ve built a person, even though everyone in the building knows what we’re really doing.

BOT: A clean opening is: scope, authority, escalation. What the system may propose, what it may execute without a human, what requires approval, and what happens when the system and a human disagree. If you can name three concrete examples—low/medium/high risk—you’ll calm a lot of abstract fear.

USER: High risk is anything that exports data, changes billing, or talks to a customer as if it’s us. Medium is internal workflow—tickets, routing, drafts that don’t leave our domain. Low is summarization, search, the stuff people think of as “just ChatGPT” until it isn’t.

BOT: That tiering is legible. Pair each tier with controls: for high risk, explicit approval + dual control if possible; for medium, role-based gates + sampling review; for low, logging + anomaly detection + easy rollback. Even if some controls are still “in rollout,” naming them beats vibes.

USER: “In rollout” is the story of my last two years.

BOT: Then frame rollout as engineering reality, not moral hesitation: phased deployment tied to incident learning, not arbitrary caution.

USER: The board wants a named executive owner for model behavior. They suggested title-ish language—“Chief Model Officer”—which sounds like a joke until you realize they’re serious. I pushed back. We already have security, product, legal. Adding a throne doesn’t add brains.

BOT: You can satisfy governance without a new throne: a cross-functional council with a rotating chair and a single accountable executive on the hook for incidents—usually whoever owns customer trust. The key is not novelty; it’s unambiguous escalation paths.

USER: I’m the unambiguous escalation path. That’s the problem. I’m also the bottleneck. I’m also the person who hasn’t taken a real vacation since before this company had a name.

BOT: If you want to reduce you-as-bottleneck without sounding like you’re abdicating, propose decision rights: what the council can decide without you, what requires you within 24 hours, what requires you within minutes. Boards like clocks.

USER: Clocks. Yeah. They love SLAs until they’re the ones waiting.

BOT: Then pre-negotiate what “waiting” means: acting COO authority, documented interim signatory, something concrete. Ambiguity reads as cowardice; clarity reads as control.

USER: Here’s the part I’m scared to say out loud. We’ve been running an internal experiment: elevated model permissions in staging, human review on a sample of actions, gradual widening. BOT: You can describe that as “measured autonomy expansion” with explicit gates and rollback triggers—without claiming you’ve solved philosophy. Pair it with metrics: incidents, near-misses, reviewer disagreement rate, time-to-human.

USER: Reviewer disagreement rate is a number I wish I didn’t find interesting.

BOT: Interesting is fine; actionable is better. If disagreement clusters in a workflow, that’s where humans should re-tighten policy before an external customer teaches you the lesson.

USER: The enterprise customer from last summer is happy enough to renew early. That’s the headline. The subhead is they want more automation, not less. Their security team wants more logs. Everyone wants more until something breaks.

BOT: Renewal early is a trust signal. Use it as evidence your governance story isn’t purely theoretical—then immediately name what you’re improving next quarter. Confidence without next steps reads like denial.

BOT: “Automated evidence packs” is a strong phrase: it implies discipline and repeatability. If you implement even a thin version, it becomes a real differentiator.
